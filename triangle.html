The Musical Triangle

COMSOL Multiphysics · Solid mechanics · Modal analysis

The orchestral triangle is a deceptively simple object: a bent steel rod, open at one corner, that rings with a bright, inharmonic spectrum. This project models the instrument in two connected ways — the forming process that leaves residual stress in the bar, and the modal behaviour that shapes the radiated sound.

Describe the open question here. For example: does the residual stress left by cold-forming the corners measurably shift the modal frequencies, or is the acoustic signature governed almost entirely by geometry and boundary conditions?

Model setup

Outline the geometry, material model, mesh, and study steps. The triangle is modelled as a slender steel rod of circular cross-section, bent into an open equilateral form. Two physics are combined: a forming/bending step using a nonlinear elastoplastic material, followed by an eigenfrequency study on the pre-stressed geometry.

A bit of the theory

The eigenfrequencies follow from the generalised eigenvalue problem of the discretised structure,

\[ \left( \mathbf{K} - \omega_i^2\, \mathbf{M} \right) \boldsymbol{\phi}_i = \mathbf{0}, \]

where \( \mathbf{K} \) and \( \mathbf{M} \) are the stiffness and mass matrices, \( \omega_i \) the angular eigenfrequency of mode \( i \), and \( \boldsymbol{\phi}_i \) the corresponding mode shape. Pre-stress enters through a geometric stiffness contribution \( \mathbf{K}_\sigma(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_0) \) that adds to \( \mathbf{K} \), so the residual field \( \boldsymbol{\sigma}_0 \) perturbs the spectrum:

\[ \left( \mathbf{K} + \mathbf{K}_\sigma(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_0) - \omega_i^2\, \mathbf{M} \right) \boldsymbol{\phi}_i = \mathbf{0}. \]
